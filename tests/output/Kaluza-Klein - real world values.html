Now if $J^\mu = \sigma E^\mu$, and $c \frac{q}{M} \rho u^\mu = J^\mu$, then $u^\mu = \frac{\sigma M}{c \rho q} E^\mu$.
And $E_\mu = (A_{\nu,\mu} - A_{\mu,\nu}) n^\nu$.
In Minkowski metric, $E_\mu = A_{0,\mu} - A_{\mu,0}$, so for $|A_{0,i}| << |A_{i,0}|$ we see that $E_i \approx -\dot{A}_i$.
So if $\dot{A}_\mu \approx -E_\mu \approx k u_\mu$ and $\dot{x}_\mu = u_\mu$ then $A_\mu \approx x_\mu$.
Also, since $u_\mu u^\mu \approx -1$, we know motion in relativity is constrained by $u_\mu x^\mu = 0$.
Therefore $A_\mu u^\mu \approx 0$.

Let's look at the magnitude of this for some real-world values.
Electrons in a copper wire (from my 'magnetic field from a boosted charge' worksheet).
$N_A = $ $\cdot \frac{1}{mol}$ = Avogadro's constant.
$m_a =$ $\frac{g}{mol} =$ {=={atomic_mass_in_kg = 1e-3 * atomic_mass_in_g}==} $ \cdot \frac{kg}{mol} = $ atomic mass.
$n_v =$ nominal valency.
$\rho =$ $\frac{g}{cm^3} =$ {=={material_density_in_kg_per_m3 = material_density_in_g_per_cm3 * 1e+3}==} $\frac{kg}{m^3} = $ matter density.
$n_e = \frac{N_A \cdot n_v}{m_a} = $ {=={free_electrons_per_kg = Avogadro_constant_in_1_per_mol * material_nominal_valency / atomic_mass_in_kg}==} $\frac{e}{kg} = $ free electrons per kg.
$\rho_{charge} = n_e \cdot \rho = $ {=={electron_density_in_e_per_m3 = free_electrons_per_kg * material_density_in_kg_per_m3}==} $\frac{e}{m^3}$ = electron density.
$R =$ $mm =$ {=={wire_radius_in_m = wire_radius_in_mm * 1e-3}==} $m =$ wire radius.
$r = \sqrt{y^2 + z^2} =$ $m = $ distance of measurement from the wire along the x-axis.
$A = \pi R^2 =$ {=={wire_cross_section_area_in_m2 = Math.PI * wire_radius_in_m * wire_radius_in_m}==} $m^2 =$ wire cross-section area
$I =$ $A =$ current in wire.
$q = \rho_{charge} \cdot e =$ {=={charge_density_in_C_per_m3 = electron_density_in_e_per_m3 * electron_charge_in_C}==} $\frac{C}{m^3} = $ charge density.
$\lambda = A q = \pi R^2 \rho_{charge} e =$ {=={charge_per_unit_length_in_C_per_m = charge_density_in_C_per_m3 * wire_cross_section_area_in_m2}==} $\frac{C}{m}$ = charge density per unit meter along wire
$v = \frac{I}{\lambda} =$ {=={mean_electron_velocity_in_m_per_s = current_in_A / (wire_cross_section_area_in_m2 * charge_density_in_C_per_m3)}==} $\frac{C}{m} =$ charge density per unit meter along wire.
$\beta = \frac{v}{c} =$ {=={Lorentz_beta = mean_electron_velocity_in_m_per_s / speed_of_light_in_m_per_s}==} = unitless, spatial component of 4-velocity.
$\gamma = 1 / \sqrt{1 - \beta^2} \approx 1 + \frac{v^2}{2 c^2} \approx $ 1 + {=={Lorentz_gamma_minus_one = .5 * Lorentz_beta * Lorentz_beta}==} $\approx$ {=={Lorentz_gamma = 1 + Lorentz_gamma_minus_one}==} = Lorentz factor.
$E = 2 \lambda k_e \frac{1}{r} \frac{v^2}{c^2} = \frac{\lambda}{2 \pi \epsilon_0 r} \frac{v^2}{c^2} = 2 \pi R^2 \rho_{charge} e k_e \frac{1}{r} =$ {=={electric_field_in_kg_m2_per_C_s2 = charge_per_unit_length_in_C_per_m * 2 * Coulomb_constant_in_kg_m3_per_C2_s2 / field_measure_distance_in_m * (mean_electron_velocity_in_m_per_s * mean_electron_velocity_in_m_per_s) / (speed_of_light_in_m_per_s * speed_of_light_in_m_per_s)}==} $\cdot \frac{kg \cdot m}{C \cdot s^2}$ = electric field magnitude.
$B = \frac{1}{c} \beta \gamma E = $ {=={magnetic_field_in_kg_m_per_C_s = Lorentz_beta * Lorentz_gamma * (electric_field_in_kg_m2_per_C_s2 / speed_of_light_in_m_per_s) }==} $\frac{kg}{C \cdot s} =$ magnetic field magnitude.
$u^0 = \gamma, u^1 = \beta \gamma, u^2 = u^3 = 0$ = our 4-velocity components, such that $\eta_{\mu\nu} u^\mu u^\nu = -1$.
$\phi_q = \frac{1}{2} \lambda k_e ln ( \frac{r}{r_0} ) =$ electric potential.
... but what is $r_0$? The EM potential has a constant which does not influence 4D or 3D EM, but will influence 5D EM.

Let's look at the potential with and without the constant: $\phi_q = \phi'_q + \phi''_q$.
$\phi'_q = \frac{1}{2} \lambda k_e ln(r) =$ {=={ electric_potential_in_kg_m2_per_C_s2 = .5 * charge_density_in_C_per_m3 * Coulomb_constant_in_kg_m3_per_C2_s2 * Math.log(field_measure_distance_in_m) }==} $\frac{kg \cdot m^2}{C \cdot s^2}$.
$\phi''_q = \frac{1}{2} \lambda k_e ln(r_0) = $ arbitrary.
$A_i = 0 = $ magnetic vector potential.
So $A_\mu u^\mu = A_0 u^0 = \frac{1}{c} \phi_q \gamma = $ {=={ A_dot_u = electric_potential_in_kg_m2_per_C_s2 / speed_of_light_in_m_per_s * Lorentz_gamma }==} $ \frac{kg \cdot m}{C \cdot s} + \frac{1}{c} \phi''_q \gamma$.
And $\frac{1}{A_5} A_\mu u^\mu = $ {=={ A_dot_u_over_A5L = A_dot_u / A5L }==} $ + \frac{1}{c A_5} \phi''_q \gamma$

Assume $u_\mu u^\mu = -1$.
$u^5 = -\frac{1}{A_5} A_\mu u^\mu = $ {=={ -A_dot_u_over_A5L }==} $ - \frac{1}{c A_5} \gamma \phi''_q$

Let's insert this into the $u^5$ equation above and solve to find what $\phi''_q$ would be:
$\frac{1}{c A_5} \gamma \phi''_q = \frac{1}{4} \frac{q}{M} \sqrt{\frac{k_e}{G}}$
$\phi''_q = \frac{1}{4} c^2 \frac{q}{\gamma m} \frac{k_e}{G} = \frac{1}{4} c^2 \frac{q}{M} \frac{k_e}{G} \sqrt{1 - \beta^2}$
Using our electron charge-to-mass ratio this gives us: $\phi''_q =$ {=={ u5U * speed_of_light_in_m_per_s * A5L / Lorentz_gamma }==} $\frac{kg \cdot m^2}{C \cdot s^2}$
...which is a few orders of magnitude higher than what contributes to the EM field.

So, assuming we want our 5-velocity to be normalized with the 5-metric, and assuming our 4-velocity is normalized with the 4-metric, then it looks like the 5th velocity is made up of two parts:
1) the EM potential, dot the negligible 4-vel, which is on the order of $10^{-12}$ for our current through a copper wire.
2) the the arbitrary constant, which must relate to the charge-mass ratio, which is on the order of $10^{21}$.
Also notice that this shows then the charge-mass ratio in the Lorentz force law will be influenced by the 3-velocity.

Of course you can avoid the constraint that that charge-mass ratio is dependent on the 3-velocity if you just relax the constraint of $u_\mu u^\mu = -1$.
Then, for constaint $u^5 = \frac{1}{4} \frac{q}{M} \sqrt{\frac{k_e}{G}}$, any deviations in the electric potential $A_t$ could relate to deviations in the 4-vel-norm (or in deviations in the Kaluza field, which I am keeping constant in this worksheet).
